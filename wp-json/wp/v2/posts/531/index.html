Here’s what you missed from the SSP Meeting in New York this week:<\/p>\n

7 Takeaways on Global Sensory Research<\/u><\/strong><\/p>\n

    \n
  1. Global research is even more complicated than you think<\/strong>. Understand the culture and\u00a0be aware of translation limitations (sometimes there really are no direct translations). Also, understand available facilities and their limitations.<\/li>\n
  2. What about multiple global data<\/strong>:\u00a0\u00a0It\u00a0is virtually impossible to conduct exact research in multiple countries. Rather, look for universal themes that can tell a story.<\/li>\n
  3. Surprising but significant obstacles <\/strong>to anticipate: Product shipments and ballot \/ survey translation and understanding.<\/li>\n
  4. Harmonization of research within multiple global sites<\/strong>:\u00a0 Some areas of research can be controlled but other areas cannot.\u00a0 Example – you can control ballot content but may not have control over testing all products or getting consumers with specific specs.<\/li>\n
  5. Reality trumps Theory<\/strong>:\u00a0 Global travelling panels to provide accurate data\u00a0seems like a good idea, but is not realistically achievable given the availability of panels.<\/li>\n
  6. Obvious but often forgotten<\/strong>:\u00a0 Learn the culture and regulations of the country in which you will be doing research.\u00a0 This avoids potential problems.<\/li>\n
  7. Set the right stage now…and later<\/strong>:\u00a0\u00a0Establish research expectations and communicate research limitations upfront.\u00a0 Know your audience and their level of understanding in sensory research and tailor presentations based on audience.<\/li>\n<\/ol>\n

    That covers the key learnings.\u00a0 You also missed some delish food, but this blog can’t help with that!<\/p>\n
